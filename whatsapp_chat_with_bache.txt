# **CHAPTER 4 – REQUIREMENTS ENGINEERING**

### *Full Explanation in Easy Words*

---

# **1. What is Requirements Engineering?**

Requirements Engineering (RE) is the process of **understanding what the customer wants** from a software system and **what limitations or rules** the system must follow.

It includes:

* Identifying system services (features)
* Identifying constraints (rules, limits)
* Writing them clearly
* Checking they are correct
* Updating when changes happen

The final output is called **System Requirements**.

---

# **2. What is a Requirement?**

A requirement can be:

* A simple, vague statement (e.g., “The system must be fast.”)
* Or a detailed technical statement (e.g., “Response time must be under 2 seconds.”)

Requirements serve two purposes:

1. **Creating project proposals** (so statements are usually flexible)
2. **Creating legal contracts** (requirements must be very detailed)

Both are still called “requirements.”

---

# **3. Types of Requirements**

### **A. User Requirements**

* Written in simple language
* For customers/end-users
* Describe *what* the users want
* No technical details

### **B. System Requirements**

* Detailed and technical
* For developers and designers
* Explains *how* the system will work
* Includes every function, rule, and constraint

---

# **4. System Stakeholders**

Stakeholders = Anyone affected by the system.

Types:

1. **End users** – People who will use the system daily
2. **System managers** – Maintain and operate the system
3. **System owners** – Pay for and control the project
4. **External stakeholders** – Government, partners, regulators, etc.

---

# **5. Functional vs Non-functional Requirements**

### **Functional Requirements**

* What the system should *do*
* Example: “System must allow login.”

Also includes how system should react to inputs and conditions.

### **Non-functional Requirements**

* Quality of the system
* Constraints on performance, security, safety, speed, usability, etc.
* Often more important because if they fail, the system may be useless

### **Domain Requirements**

* Rules from the industry/environment
* Example: Banking system must follow financial regulations.

---

# **6. Problems in Requirements**

### **Imprecision (Not clear enough)**

If functional requirements are unclear, users and developers can interpret them differently → leads to mistakes.

### **Completeness**

All required features must be included.

### **Consistency**

No contradictions between requirements.

In real life, **perfect completeness and consistency are almost impossible**.

---

# **7. Types of Non-functional Requirements**

### **A. Product Requirements**

How the system should behave
Examples:

* Reliability
* Speed
* Performance

### **B. Organisational Requirements**

Based on company policies
Examples:

* Must use a specific programming language
* Must follow ISO standards

### **C. External Requirements**

Based on outside rules
Examples:

* Government laws
* Data privacy rules
* Industry standards

---

# **8. Metrics for Non-functional Requirements**

Non-functional requirements should be measurable.

Examples:

* “System must respond in < 2 seconds.”
* “Uptime must be 99.9%.”

---

# **9. Requirements Engineering Process**

Every RE process includes 4 major activities:

1. **Requirements Elicitation** – Finding out what users want
2. **Requirements Analysis** – Understanding and organizing requirements
3. **Requirements Validation** – Checking if requirements are correct
4. **Requirements Management** – Controlling changes over time

This process is **iterative (repeats like a cycle/spiral).**

---

# **10. Requirements Elicitation & Analysis**

Also called **requirements discovery**.

Engineers work with stakeholders to:

* Learn domain knowledge
* Find required services
* Understand constraints
* Discover conflicts and missing information

Stakeholders include:

* Users
* Managers
* Domain experts
* Maintenance teams
* External agencies

---

# **11. Steps in Elicitation & Analysis**

1. **Discovery**
   Collecting raw requirements from stakeholders.

2. **Classification & Organization**
   Grouping similar requirements.

3. **Prioritization & Negotiation**
   Deciding which requirements are important and resolving conflicts.

4. **Specification**
   Writing requirements clearly in documents.

---

# **12. Requirements Specification**

This is the step where all requirements are **written down clearly**.

Includes:

* User requirements (simple language)
* System requirements (technical details)

It is often part of the **contract** between client and developer.

---

# **13. Ways of Writing Requirements**

### 1. **Structured Specifications**

* Uses fixed formats or templates
* Reduces confusion
* Good for technical/embedded systems
* Not very flexible for business systems

### 2. **Tabular Specifications**

* Useful for multiple conditions and actions
* Example: Insulin pump calculations in a table

### 3. **Use Cases**

* Part of UML
* Describe the interaction between user and system
* Include:

  * Actors (users)
  * Steps of interaction
* Can be expanded using sequence diagrams

---

# **14. Software Requirements Document (SRS)**

SRS is the **official document** that describes everything the system must do.

Contains:

* User requirements
* System requirements

Important:
SRS describes **WHAT**, not **HOW** the system should be built.

Users of SRS include:

* Customers
* Managers
* Designers
* Testers
* Maintainers

Structure often includes:

* Introduction
* General description
* Specific requirements
* Appendices
* Index

---

# **15. Requirements Validation**

Ensuring that requirements truly represent what the customer wants.

Why?
Because fixing a requirements error later can cost **up to 100x more**.

### Validation Checks:

* **Validity:** Do they solve the customer’s problem?
* **Consistency:** No conflicts?
* **Completeness:** Nothing missing?
* **Realism:** Can it be implemented?
* **Verifiability:** Can we test it?

### Validation Techniques:

1. Reviews (manual checking)
2. Prototyping
3. Test-case generation

---

# **16. Requirements Change**

Requirements often change because:

* Business environment changes
* New hardware/software appears
* User needs change
* Laws or regulations change
* Different user groups have conflicting needs

Large systems always go through change.

---

# **17. Requirements Change Management**

3-step process:

### **1. Problem Analysis & Change Specification**

* Understand the request
* Check if it is valid
* Clarify the change

### **2. Change Analysis & Costing**

* Evaluate impact
* Check related requirements (traceability)
* Decide if change should be accepted

### **3. Change Implementation**

* Update requirements document
* Update design & code (if needed)

Documents must be organized so updates are easy.

# **Chapter 5 – System Modeling (FULL DETAILED EXPLANATION)**

## **1. What is System Modeling? (In Depth)**

System modeling is the process of **creating simplified representations (models)** of a real system before actually building it.

### Why we do system modeling?

* Real systems are **too complex**
* Models help us:

  * Understand how the system should work
  * Find errors early
  * Explain ideas to customers and team members
  * Improve communication between developers and stakeholders

### What is a model?

A **model** is:

* Not the real system
* A **simplified picture**
* Focuses only on **important details**

Example:
Before constructing a building, engineers draw **blueprints**.
Similarly, before developing software, engineers create **system models**.

---

## **2. Use of UML in System Modeling**

Most system models are created using **UML (Unified Modeling Language)**.

### Why UML?

* UML is a **standard language**
* Easy to understand globally
* Uses diagrams instead of long text
* Supports different system views

UML helps:

* Analysts understand system functionality
* Developers design systems
* Customers visualize the system

---

Planned system models are used **throughout software development**, especially during **requirements engineering**.

### During Requirements Engineering:

* Models help explain:

  * What the system will do
  * What it will not do
* Stakeholders can:

  * Review requirements
  * Suggest changes

### During Design:

* Engineers discuss:

  * System structure
  * Possible solutions
* Models act as **design documents**

### During Implementation:

* In **Model-Driven Engineering**, models can:

  * Automatically generate code
  * Reduce manual coding
  * Reduce errors

👉 Models are not just drawings — they guide the **entire development process**.

# **Chapter 8: Software Testing – Long Question Explanation**

---

## **1. Software Testing**

**Software testing** is the process of executing a program or system with the intention of **finding errors and defects** before the software is delivered to the end user. The main objective of testing is to ensure that the software **behaves according to its specifications** and satisfies user requirements.

During testing, the software is executed using **artificial or real test data**, and the output is examined to identify:

* Errors
* Defects
* Unexpected behavior
* Non-functional issues such as performance or reliability problems

An important principle of software testing is that **testing can reveal the presence of defects, but it can never prove their absence**. Testing is therefore a **risk-reduction activity**, not a guarantee of perfection.

Software testing is part of a broader process known as **Verification and Validation (V&V)**, which also includes **static techniques** such as reviews and inspections .

---

## **2. Verification**

**Verification** answers the question:

> **“Are we building the product right?”**

Verification focuses on checking whether each phase of development correctly implements the output of the previous phase. It ensures that the software is being built **according to design and specifications**.

### **Key Characteristics of Verification**

* Performed mainly by **developers**
* Concerned with **phase containment of errors**
* Ensures correctness of intermediate artifacts

### **Verification Techniques**

* Reviews
* Inspections
* Unit Testing
* Integration Testing

Verification activities are mostly **static**, meaning the software may not be executed during these checks .

---

## **3. Validation**

**Validation** answers the question:

> **“Are we building the right product?”**

Validation ensures that the **final software product meets user requirements** and conforms to the **Software Requirements Specification (SRS)**.

### **Key Characteristics of Validation**

* Performed mainly by **testers**
* Focuses on the **final product**
* Ensures the system is **fit for use**

### **Validation Technique**

* System Testing

Validation is mainly **dynamic**, meaning the software is executed and tested with real or simulated data .

---

## **4. Types of Testing (Based on Levels)**

Software testing can be categorized into the following levels:

### **1. Unit Testing**

* Performed during the **development phase**
* Tests individual program units or modules

### **2. Integration Testing**

* Performed after unit testing
* Tests interaction between modules

### **3. System Testing**

* Tests the complete integrated system
* Performed during testing phase

### **4. Regression Testing**

* Performed during **maintenance**
* Ensures new changes do not break existing functionality .

---

## **5. Functional and Non-Functional Requirements Testing**

* **Functional requirements** are tested at **all testing levels**
* **Non-functional requirements** (performance, security, reliability) are tested mainly during **system testing**

This distinction ensures that both **what the system does** and **how well it does it** are validated .

---

## **6. Unit Testing**

**Unit testing** is the process of testing the **smallest testable parts of a program**, such as functions, methods, or classes, to ensure that they work correctly in isolation.

### **Characteristics**

* Performed by **developers**
* Focuses on **internal logic**
* Detects errors early
* Usually automated

Unit testing ensures that each unit behaves correctly before integration with other units .

---

## **7. Integration Testing**

**Integration testing** verifies that **individual modules work correctly together** after unit testing.

### **Objectives**

* Detect interface defects
* Validate data flow between modules
* Ensure correct interaction

### **Types of Integration Testing**

1. **Big Bang Integration**
2. **Top-Down Integration**
3. **Bottom-Up Integration**

Integration testing occurs **after unit testing and before system testing** .

---

## **8. System Testing**

**System testing** validates the **complete and fully integrated software system** against the specified requirements.

### **Characteristics**

* Black-box testing
* End-to-end validation
* Focuses on user requirements

### **Based on Who Performs Testing**

* **Alpha Testing** – by developers
* **Beta Testing** – by friends or limited users
* **Acceptance Testing** – by actual users

---

## **9. System Testing Based on Non-Functional Requirements**

### **1. Performance Testing**

* Evaluates speed, scalability, and stability

### **2. Load Testing**

* Tests behavior under expected and peak loads

### **3. Stress Testing**

* Pushes system beyond limits to test robustness and recovery

### **4. Security Testing**

* Tests system behavior under attacks

These tests ensure the system performs well under real-world conditions .

---

## **10. Black-Box Testing**

**Black-box testing** focuses on testing the **external behavior** of the system without knowing its internal code or structure.

### **Characteristics**

* Tester has no knowledge of source code
* Based on requirements and specifications
* Focuses on input-output behavior

---

## **11. Boundary Value Testing**

Boundary Value Testing is a black-box technique that focuses on **testing at the boundaries of input ranges**, where errors are most likely to occur.

Example:

* If valid speed range is 40–80, test values: 39, 40, 41, 79, 80, 81

This technique increases defect detection efficiency .

---

## **12. White-Box Testing**

**White-box testing** examines the **internal structure and logic** of the program.

### **Also Known As**

* Clear-box testing
* Glass-box testing
* Structural testing

### **Applied At**

* Unit level
* Integration level
* System level

Requires complete knowledge of source code .

---

## **13. White-Box Testing Techniques**

### **1. Control Flow Testing**

Uses **Control Flow Graph (CFG)** to verify execution paths and logical correctness.

### **2. Data Flow Testing**

Examines how variables are defined and used to detect anomalies.

### **Advantages**

* Detects unused variables
* Finds uninitialized variables
* Identifies incorrect variable usage

### **3. Branch Testing**

Ensures **every branch in the code is executed at least once**.

### **Why Branch Testing is Needed**

* Ensures no branch causes abnormal behavior
* Guarantees full branch coverage
* Measures code coverage

---

## **14. Statement Coverage Testing**

Statement coverage ensures that **every executable statement in the program is executed at least once**.

### **Purpose**

* Detect dead code
* Identify unused statements
* Improve test completeness

---

## **15. Decision Coverage Testing**

Decision coverage ensures that **each decision point** (if, while, switch) is evaluated for:

* True condition
* False condition

It is **stronger than statement coverage** and improves logical correctness .

---

## **Conclusion**

Software testing is a **systematic and disciplined process** that ensures software quality, reliability, and correctness. By applying different testing levels and techniques such as **unit testing, integration testing, system testing, black-box testing, and white-box testing**, defects can be detected early and software quality can be significantly improved.

## **9. Lines of Code (LOC)**

Not included:

### **Advantages**

### **Disadvantages**

* Ignores complexity and quality

Because of these limitations, LOC alone is insufficient.

## **10. Function Points (FP)**

### **Definition**

Function Points measure software size **from the user’s perspective**, focusing on **functionality delivered**, not code size.

### **Functional Units**

1. External Inputs (EI)
2. External Outputs (EO)
3. External Inquiries (EQ)
4. Internal Logical Files (ILF)
5. External Interface Files (EIF)

---

## **11. Function Point Calculation Steps**

### **Step 1: Complexity Classification**

Each functional unit is classified as:

* Low
* Average
* High

### **Step 2: Unadjusted Function Points (UFP)**

UFP is calculated by:

* Counting functional units
* Multiplying by corresponding weights

### **Step 3: Technical Complexity Factor (TCF)**

TCF is calculated using **14 technical factors**, each rated from **0 to 5**.

Final Formula:
[
FP = UFP \times (0.65 + 0.01 \times \sum Fi)
]

## **12. Advantages and Disadvantages of Function Points**

### **Advantages**

* Can be estimated early
* Language independent
* More accurate than LOC

### **Disadvantages**

* Subjective
* Difficult to automate
* Ignores output quality

## **13. COCOMO (Constructive Cost Model)**

COCOMO is a **software cost estimation model developed by Barry Boehm in 1981**.
It estimates **effort, schedule, staffing, and productivity** based on:

* Software size (KLOC)
* Cost drivers

---

## **14. Software Development Modes in COCOMO**

### **1. Organic Mode**

* Small, simple projects
* Experienced team
* Stable environment
* Size: 0–50 KLOC

### **2. Semi-Detached Mode**

* Medium complexity
* Mixed experience team
* Size: 50–300 KLOC

### **3. Embedded Mode**

* High complexity
* Tight constraints
* High innovation
* Size: >300 KLOC

---

## **15. Basic COCOMO Model**

### **Effort Formula**

[
Effort = a_b \times (KLOC)^{b_b}
]

### **Development Time**

[
Time = c_b \times (Effort)^{d_b}
]

### **Staffing**

[
Staff = Effort / Time
]

### **Productivity**

[
Productivity = KLOC / Effort
]

Used for **quick and rough estimates**.

---

## **16. Intermediate COCOMO Model**

The Intermediate COCOMO model improves accuracy by using **15 cost drivers**, grouped as:

* Product attributes
* Computer attributes
* Personnel attributes
* Project attributes

### **Effort Formula**

[
Effort = a_i \times (KLOC)^{b_i} \times EAF
]

Where:

* EAF = Effort Adjustment Factor

# 🔵 PRODUCT LIFE CYCLE (PLC)

**Product Life Cycle** shows that **every product passes through stages** from launch to exit.

The **4 stages** are:
1️⃣ Introduction
2️⃣ Growth
3️⃣ Maturity
4️⃣ Decline

---

## 1️⃣ INTRODUCTION STAGE

### 📘 Meaning:

The product is **newly launched** in the market.

### Characteristics:

* **Sales:** Low (people don’t know the product)
* **Costs:** High (advertising, promotion)
* **Profits:** Negative or very low
* **Customers:** Innovators (people who try new things)
* **Competitors:** Few

### Marketing Objective:

* Create **awareness and trial**

### Strategies:

* Offer a **basic product**
* High price (skimming) OR low price (penetration)
* Selective distribution
* Heavy promotion to inform customers

📌 Example:
New smartphone launch

🧠 Easy line:

> Introduction = **new product, high cost, low sales**

---

## 2️⃣ GROWTH STAGE

### 📘 Meaning:

Product becomes **popular** and sales increase fast.

### Characteristics:

* **Sales:** Rapidly rising
* **Costs:** Average
* **Profits:** Rising
* **Customers:** Early adopters
* **Competitors:** Increasing

### Marketing Objective:

* **Maximize market share**

### Strategies:

* Improve product (features, service, warranty)
* Price to penetrate market
* Intensive distribution
* Strong advertising

📌 Example:
Smartphones becoming common

🧠 Easy line:

> Growth = **fast sales, rising profits**

---

## 3️⃣ MATURITY STAGE

### 📘 Meaning:

Product reaches **peak sales** and market becomes saturated.

### Characteristics:

* **Sales:** Peak / stable
* **Costs:** Low per customer
* **Profits:** High
* **Customers:** Middle majority
* **Competitors:** Stable or beginning to decline

### Marketing Objective:

* Maximize profit and **defend market share**

### Strategies:

* Diversify brand and models
* Price to match or beat competitors
* Very strong distribution
* Stress brand differences and benefits

📌 Example:
Televisions, refrigerators

🧠 Easy line:

> Maturity = **maximum sales and profits**

---

## 4️⃣ DECLINE STAGE

### 📘 Meaning:

Product sales start **falling** due to new technology or changing tastes.

### Characteristics:

* **Sales:** Declining
* **Costs:** Low
* **Profits:** Declining
* **Customers:** Laggards
* **Competitors:** Decreasing

### Marketing Objective:

* Reduce cost and **milk the brand**

### Strategies:

* Cut price
* Reduce outlets
* Phase out weak products
* Minimal promotion

📌 Example:
DVD players, landline phones

🧠 Easy line:

> Decline = **falling sales and profits**

# 📘 LESSON 3: PLACE (DISTRIBUTION)

## 🔵 What is “Place” in Marketing?

In the **marketing mix (4Ps)**, **Place** means:

👉 **How a product moves from the producer (company) to the final customer**

It is also called a **distribution channel**.

📌 Example:
Factory → warehouse → shop → customer

🧠 Easy line:

> Place = **how and where customers get the product**

---

## 🔵 What is a Distribution Channel?

### Definition (Easy):

A **distribution channel** is the **path and activities** used to move a product from the **producer to the consumer**.

It includes:

* Transportation
* Storage
* Selling
* Intermediaries (middlemen)

### Intermediaries are:

* Wholesalers
* Transporters
* Retailers
* Warehouses

🧠 Easy line:

> Distribution channel = **the road a product travels to reach customers**

---

# 🔵 TYPES OF DISTRIBUTION CHANNELS

There are **4 main types**:

---

## 1️⃣ DIRECT DISTRIBUTION

### Meaning:

The **producer sells directly to the consumer**.

### No middlemen involved.

📌 Example:

* Company website selling directly
* Farmer selling vegetables directly

🧠 Easy line:

> Direct channel = **no middleman**

---

## 2️⃣ INDIRECT DISTRIBUTION

### Meaning:

The producer uses **one or more intermediaries**.

### Flow:

Producer → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer

📌 Example:

* FMCG products in supermarkets

🧠 Easy line:

> Indirect channel = **middlemen involved**

---

## 3️⃣ DUAL DISTRIBUTION

### Meaning:

Company uses **both direct and indirect channels** at the same time.

📌 Example:

* Nike sells online (direct)
* Nike sells in stores (indirect)

🧠 Easy line:

> Dual distribution = **direct + indirect**

---

## 4️⃣ REVERSE DISTRIBUTION CHANNEL

### Meaning:

Product moves **from consumer back to producer**.

📌 Example:

* Recycling bottles
* Returning old electronics
* Trade-in programs

🧠 Easy line:

> Reverse channel = **customer sends product back**

---

# 🔵 TYPES OF INTERMEDIARIES (MIDDLEMEN)

There are **4 main intermediaries**:

---

## 1️⃣ AGENTS

### Meaning:

Agents **represent the producer** but **do not own the product**.

### Key points:

* Independent
* Earn **commission or fees**
* Do not buy products

📌 Example:

* Real estate agent
* Sales agent

🧠 Easy line:

> Agent = **seller without ownership**

---

## 2️⃣ WHOLESALERS

### Meaning:

Wholesalers **buy goods in bulk** from producers.

### What they do:

* Store goods in warehouses
* Sell in smaller quantities
* Sell to retailers or other intermediaries

📌 Example:

* Rice wholesaler
* Grocery wholesaler

🧠 Easy line:

> Wholesaler = **buys big, sells small**

---

## 3️⃣ DISTRIBUTORS

### Meaning:

Similar to wholesalers but with **one difference**.

### Key difference:

* Distributor sells **only one brand**
* Wholesaler sells **many brands**

📌 Example:

* Authorized Samsung distributor

🧠 Easy line:

> Distributor = **one brand specialist**

---

## 4️⃣ RETAILERS

### Meaning:

Retailers sell products to the **final consumer**.

### Examples:

* Supermarkets
* Shops
* Online stores

🧠 Easy line:

> Retailer = **last link before customer**

---

# 🔵 SUPPLY CHAIN

## What is a Supply Chain?

### Definition (Easy):

A **supply chain** is the **entire system** that moves a product from **raw materials to final customer**.

It includes:

* Suppliers
* Manufacturers
* Warehouses
* Transport
* Retailers

🧠 Easy line:

> Supply chain = **everything from raw material to customer**

---

## 🔵 SUPPLY CHAIN MANAGEMENT (SCM)

### Meaning:

SCM means **planning and managing** all activities in the supply chain.

Includes:

* Buying raw materials
* Production
* Storage
* Transportation
* Coordination with partners

🧠 Easy line:

> SCM = **managing the whole flow efficiently**

---

# 🔵 SUPPLY CHAIN vs DISTRIBUTION CHANNEL

### Key Difference:

| Supply Chain              | Distribution Channel          |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Starts from raw materials | Focuses on customer delivery  |
| Broader concept           | Narrower concept              |
| Internally focused        | Customer focused              |
| Efficiency & cost         | Value & customer satisfaction |

🧠 Easy line:

> Supply chain = **inside the company**
> Distribution channel = **towards customer**

---

## 🔵 MARKETING VIEW vs SUPPLY CHAIN VIEW

* **Marketers** see distribution as part of **4Ps**
* **Supply chain professionals** focus on **efficiency & cost**
* Marketers may keep a less efficient channel if it gives **better customer value**

📌 Example:
A retailer that promotes well but is costly may still be kept.

🧠 Easy line:

> Marketing balances **efficiency + customer value**

---

# 🧠 FINAL EASY SUMMARY (EXAM READY)

* Place means **how products reach customers**
* Distribution channels move products from **producer to consumer**
* Channels can be **direct, indirect, dual, or reverse**
* Intermediaries include **agents, wholesalers, distributors, retailers**
* Supply chain is **broader** than distribution channels
* Distribution channels are **customer-facing**

**Q1. What type of advertising platform is Google Ads?**

A) Subscription-based advertising
B) Pay-per-click (PPC) advertising
C) Cost-per-impression advertising
D) Flat-rate advertising

**Answer: B**
*Google Ads is a PPC platform where advertisers pay only when users click their ads.*

---

**Q2. How many primary advertising channels does the Google Ads ecosystem have?**

A) Three
B) Four
C) Five
D) Six

**Answer: D**
*The six channels are: Search Network, Display Network, YouTube, Shopping Ads, App Campaigns, and Performance Max.*

---

**Q3. Which three components does Google evaluate during an ad auction?**

A) Budget, keywords, and location
B) Advertiser's bid, quality score, and ad rank
C) Click-through rate, impressions, and conversions
D) Headline, description, and landing page

**Answer: B**
*Google uses the advertiser's bid, quality score, and ad rank to determine which ads are shown.*

---

**Q4. What does a higher Quality Score generally result in?**

A) Higher advertising costs and worse ad positions
B) More impressions but fewer clicks
C) Lower advertising costs and better ad positions
D) Higher bids required to compete

**Answer: C**
*A higher quality score rewards advertisers with lower costs and better ad placement.*

---

**Q5. What is the correct Google Ads account hierarchy?**

A) Ad groups → Campaigns → Accounts
B) Accounts → Ad groups → Campaigns
C) Campaigns → Accounts → Ad groups
D) Accounts → Campaigns → Ad groups

**Answer: D**
*The hierarchy flows from Account at the top, then Campaigns, then Ad Groups containing keywords and ads.*

---

**Q6. How are keywords typically categorized in Google Ads?**

A) Positive and negative keywords
B) Short-tail and long-tail keywords
C) Primary and secondary keywords
D) Branded and non-branded keywords

**Answer: B**
*Short-tail keywords are broad and competitive; long-tail keywords are specific with lower competition.*

---

**Q7. Which keyword match type provides the MOST precise targeting?**

A) Broad match
B) Modified broad match
C) Phrase match
D) Exact match

**Answer: D**
*Exact match shows ads only when searches closely match the keyword, giving the highest precision.*

---

**Q8. What is the purpose of negative keywords?**

A) To increase ad reach to a broader audience
B) To improve quality score automatically
C) To eliminate irrelevant searches and protect budget
D) To target users who previously visited your site

**Answer: C**
*Negative keywords prevent ads from appearing on irrelevant searches, saving budget and improving accuracy.*

---

**Q9. Which is NOT a key element of an effective Google Ads landing page?**

A) Strong headline
B) Clear benefits
C) Customer testimonials
D) Multiple navigation menus

**Answer: D**
*Multiple navigation menus distract visitors. Effective pages use a headline, benefits, testimonials, and a clear call to action.*

---

**Q10. What are Responsive Search Ads designed to do?**

A) Automatically adjust bids based on audience
B) Only show ads on mobile devices
C) Allow multiple headlines and descriptions to be tested for best performance
D) Target users based on browsing history

**Answer: C**
*Responsive Search Ads let Google test multiple headline and description combinations to find the best-performing version.*

---

**Q11. What does "search intent" refer to in Google Ads?**

A) The geographic location of the user
B) The device a user is searching from
C) The time of day a user searches
D) The user's purpose behind their search query

**Answer: D**
*Search intent can be informational, commercial, or transactional. Aligning ads with intent increases engagement and conversions.*

---

**Q12. Which campaign type uses AI to automate delivery across all Google channels?**

A) Search Network campaigns
B) Display Network campaigns
C) Shopping campaigns
D) Performance Max campaigns

**Answer: D**
*Performance Max uses AI and audience signals to automate campaign delivery across all of Google's channels.*

---

**Q13. What does Click-Through Rate (CTR) measure?**

A) How many times an ad is shown
B) How often users click an ad after seeing it
C) The percentage of visitors who convert
D) The relevance of an ad to a keyword

**Answer: B**
*CTR = clicks ÷ impressions. It's a key indicator of how relevant and compelling your ad is to users.*

---

**Q14. What is remarketing in Google Ads?**

A) Targeting new audiences who have never heard of your brand
B) Bidding on competitor brand keywords
C) Running ads only on the Search Network
D) Targeting users who have previously engaged with your brand

**Answer: D**
*Remarketing re-engages familiar audiences who visited your site or interacted with your brand, boosting conversion chances.*

---

**Q15. Which of the following is a common mistake advertisers make in Google Ads?**

A) Using too many negative keywords
B) Targeting too narrow an audience
C) Neglecting conversion tracking and using poorly designed landing pages
D) Tracking conversions too frequently

**Answer: C**
*Common pitfalls include neglecting conversion tracking, targeting too broadly, and using ineffective landing pages — all of which hurt campaign performance.*
